(3 comments)

Neil, James, I was thinking that wait_for_completion() might be better (at least a more clear implementation), but reading Documentation//scheduler/completion.rst indicates that only a single thread can call wait_for_completion() for each complete() call, or it will block.

What we really want is a single complete() for this data structure, and any number of threads that call wait_for_completion() to block until complete(), and then never care about it for later checks, so it doesn't seem to match the semantics of wait_for_completion().

--- Message (Neil Brown, 2020-03-18 22:37:28.000000000) ---
Patch Set 1: Code-Review-1

I'm missing some background here so it is hard to analyse the patch properly, but it looks strange.

I would like to suggest that we don't need a new field, just use wait_on_var_event() to wait for typ_lu to become non-NULL.  But it may be that sometimes it is expected to remain NULL.  Certainly  obd_setup seems to expect that it could be NULL.

Also obd_setup() loads a value from ->typ_lu, then waits for an event that happens just after typ_lu gets set.  So if it needed to wait, it will have an out-of-date value.

W.r.t completions, it is probably helpful to know that they were designed to overcome a weakness with semaphores.
The old practice was to 'down' a semaphore, perform some action, then 'up' it.  A waiting thread could choose to 'down' the semaphore and so wait for the action to complete.  That had problems if the semaphore was freed immediately after the 'down' succeeded.  The 'up' might still make another access to the semaphore. So the 'completion' was invented where signalling completion was the very last thing it did.
The point being that the one-waiter model was a strong assumption in the initial design.

I think wait_var_event() is the right interface when several threads might be waiting for something.  It just isn't clear to me from the patch what the legal states for "typ_lu" are and what they mean.
