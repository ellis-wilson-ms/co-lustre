Its not about correctness but about noise on the system which I know is really hard for people outside of our centers to wrap their heads around. Noise is super important for DOE sites and I will give you examples of how we go out of way to avoid it.

With new systems we profile hardware to make sure the variable is within 5% of baseline. For example with our large storage clusters we take our 20000+ hard drives and measure the low level block performance to gather a baseline and then look to see which drives have variability outside of the 5% range. Normally about a 1/3 of the HDs have to be replaced. SSD are treated the same. We also consider various other factors which is outside this discussion.

A good example dealing with Lustre servers is that noise impacts what hardware we pick. The noise pollution is so bad with Lustre on servers we can not deploy server hardware that has NUMA nodes. It just has such a severe impact across our center. We have a really bad work around, limited hardware selection, but its not optimal. Its something to really explore in the upcoming year. It will also make an excellent research paper. For example I did this paper several years ago about this topic related to the Gemini LND driver:

https://cug.org/proceedings/cug2013_proceedings/includes/files/pap108.pdf

Our clients do have NUMA nodes and we end up partitioning the setup to minimize the impact on applications. Cray systems even offer the ability to isolate all IRQs to one core that user land can avoid. Several times in the past a study has been done to determine which IRQ is the greatest offender. We found (80+% of IRQs) has been due to the PIT timer which is what jiffies uses on x86 platforms for its clock source. Additional the drift of the PIT timer also has caused us headaches but that is another issue.

This is one of the reason we have been exploring tick less systems which works with the idea of isolating the PIT timer to a single core. With most application being GPU based today many of cores have limited use these days outside of being used to drive I/O. This means we can power down our idle cores which helps us meet our green requirements. Now if PIT timer IRQs going off this tends to impact our ability to idle our cores. 

We can use jiffies but it requires real thought about the impact. This means anything using jiffies has to be run on the same core that the PIT timer IRQs occur on and use the proper NUMA node to minimize the impact to the system. Now you might think this is extreme but then you could say the same thing about the complex NUMA management in the LNet layer that tries to resolve similar problems. If this is not done properly it does end up costing our users extra time to complete their projects and extra $$$. This also does impact our energy bill as well. That is extra money that could be used to buy more DDN hardware :-) So I like you to really consider this as a customer. Now you can ignore this concern and that is fine since it wouldn't be the first time a vendor has ignored us :-( Its really something to ponder.

I think next year I'm going to do a talk about noise at LUG. I hope this explains why I seem extreme about this.

> Its not about correctness but about noise on the system

Sure, I understand the concern with jitter on HPC systems, which is why the timeout_round() function existed in the first place to group the wakeups to a single tick rather than smearing them across every msec and continually interrupting the job.  Unfortunately that optimization was removed by your earlier timer patch... :-/

I think it would be even better (if client clocks are synchronized across clients) to align the wakeups across all nodes so that only a single application timestep is affected by the housekeeping tasks (see my comments in LU-9441 about this).

On the flip side, there have been a number of different bugs introduced by the conversion from jiffies to time64_t and ktime_t, so I'm cautious about making major changes unless there is real value.  I agree that there are improvements that can be made, and I'm happy to see them, but in hindsight it seems there are lots of minefields in this code and we need to be careful.

I think the first step would be to reintroduce timeout_round() to the places where it was previously used, and also see what other timers are causing wakeups, and then look into what can be done to reduce frequency or improve efficiency of that code.  I think LU-9441 might be the right place for tracking that work.

--- Message (Andreas Dilger, 2019-11-08 05:27:12.000000000) ---
Patch Set 1:

> If the code uses cfs_time_seconds at all, then it is working in
 > jiffies.  If it uses mod_timer(), then the timer is set in jiffies.
 > If using jiffies causes jitter, then this code will cause jitter.

It's not *just* that using jiffies causes jitter, it is the randomness of the wakeups/slowdown in the computation that causes jitter.  In a large HPC application, there might be 1M threads computing and exchanging messages in parallel, in some cases thousands of times per second, and the calculation cannot continue until all of the computation and communication for that time-step is finished (i.e. global barrier on each time-step).

While wakeups with HZ=100 or HZ=1000 to do occasional background work might seem like a low frequency to be interrupted to do some "unimportant" work (i.e. keep the filesystem/kernel working), across thousands of nodes and hundreds of timers, that means there is a steady stream of wakeups, each wakeup making a *different* time-step slower (say slowdown=10x for a random 10ms slowdown with HZ=1000).  In essence, the whole computation will run at the slowest speed of *any* thread on any node (i.e. globally 10x slower than peak speed).

When we used round_timeout(), it would batch at least the Lustre wakeups on a node to be aligned on jiffies that are an even multiple of HZ, so that there are fewer timer interrupts (1/HZ as many).  Having the background work all be in the same time-step means only (1/HZ) of time-steps are slow (i.e. only (1000 + 10)/1000 = 0.01x slower).

 > Storing timeouts in second or nanoseconds instead of jiffies isn't
 > going to reduce jitter  - you would need to use hires-timers so
 > that the timeout you request if in nanoseconds would be the only
 > way to avoid jitter.

My proposal (described briefly in LU-9441) would be to use e.g. the wallclock time (aligned by NTP) to have the wakeups across *all* nodes to happen at the same time.  That ensures the *same* global 1% or 0.1% of time-steps are slow and 99% or 99.9% are fast across all nodes, which helps the global calculation even more.
