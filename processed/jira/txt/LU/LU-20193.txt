Per-Target (OST/MDT) Bandwidth Control for Lustre NRS TBF

This proposal introduces a new storage target classification type for the Lustre NRS TBF scheduler, enabling per‑target (OST/MDT) bandwidth control instead of only per‑service (OSS/MDS‑wide) control. With this enhancement, administrators can write rules such as:

nrs_tbf_rule="start r1 ost={1 2} rate=500"

to explicitly constrain the bandwidth available to I/O requests targeting OST1 and OST2 to a configured rate (e.g., 500 RPCs/sec), regardless of the aggregate capacity of the entire OSS.

Motivation

On modern high‑performance Lustre systems, a single OSS typically hosts multiple OSTs. A TBF scheduling partition (service part) therefore receives I/O requests from many clients, directed at different underlying OSTs. Today, TBF typically estimates capacity and rate limits at the OSS level, using an aggregate view of all attached OSTs.

In practice, however, client load is often unevenly distributed across OSTs:

Some OSTs may be heavily used, while others remain mostly idle.

Clients or higher‑level striping policies might direct most traffic to only a subset of OSTs.

If the scheduler continues to base its decisions on the aggregate bandwidth of all OSTs on an OSS, it may overestimate the available bandwidth capacity for workloads that hit only one or a few OSTs. This can lead to:

Localized overload on those few OSTs (e.g., OST 1 and 2), even though the overall OSS still appears to be within its "aggregate bandwidth budget".

Unfair or unstable performance, where some clients suffer on hot OSTs while others see underutilized capacity on idle OSTs.

Per‑OST/MDT Control via Storage Target Type

By introducing a storage target type into the TBF classifier, rules can directly express which OST/MDT indices they apply to. For example:

ost={1 2} — apply this rule to I/O directed to OST1 and OST2.

ost={3} — a separate rule for OST3, potentially with a different rate.

mdt={1} — a separate rule for MDT1, mainly used for metadata operations.

No ost/mdt specified — rule continues to act on the default (aggregate) dimension, preserving current behavior.

This allows an administrator to:

Set distinct bandwidth limits or reservations per OST/MDT.

Tune each OST/MDT according to its measured or expected capacity, which is generally much easier and more accurate than reasoning about the combined behavior of many OSTs/MDTs.

Avoid relying on a single "aggregate OSS capacity" when real‑world load patterns only exercise a subset of OSTs.

Benefits

The main benefits of this design are:

More accurate capacity modeling

Each OST’s bandwidth capability can be measured and modeled separately, and TBF limits can be aligned with those per‑OST capabilities instead of a coarse OSS‑wide average.

Improved fairness and stability

When traffic is concentrated on a small number of OSTs, per‑OST limits prevent a single hot OST from being driven well beyond its sustainable throughput just because other OSTs happen to be idle.

Better utilization of hardware

Because decisions are made at the OST granularity, operators can:

	Protect weaker OSTs (e.g., slower media, older hardware) with tighter caps.

	Allow higher limits on faster OSTs, without having to compromise on a single OSS‑wide number.

Summary

In summary, adding an storage target classification type and allowing TBF rules like "ost={1 2} rate=500" makes it possible to bind bandwidth limits directly to specific OSTs. This aligns the scheduler’s view of capacity with how performance is actually constrained in the hardware, leading to more predictable and controllable bandwidth allocation on multi‑OST OSS servers.
